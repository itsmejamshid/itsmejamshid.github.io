Learning in Public

July 20, 2025 6 min read
Learning Community Growth

Why sharing your learning journey—the struggles, breakthroughs, and half-formed thoughts—can accelerate growth and help others. On building a practice of documenting knowledge, asking questions openly, and turning curiosity into community.

The Fear of Being Wrong

Most developers I know have a secret fear: someone will discover they don't know as much as they seem to. This impostor syndrome keeps us quiet about our learning process, afraid to ask questions or share incomplete thoughts. We wait until we feel "expert enough" to contribute publicly.

But here's the thing: that day never comes. There's always more to learn, always someone who knows more. The goal isn't to be perfect—it's to be helpful and to grow.

"The best way to learn is to teach."

What Learning in Public Looks Like

Learning in public isn't about broadcasting every Google search or live-tweeting your debugging sessions (though sometimes that can be helpful). It's about making your learning process visible and shareable. This might include:

  • Writing about problems you're solving, even if you haven't solved them yet
  • Sharing code snippets that helped you understand a concept
  • Documenting the resources that clicked for you
  • Asking questions on forums, Twitter, or Slack channels
  • Building small projects to explore new technologies
  • Contributing to open source projects, even in small ways

The key is consistency, not perfection. A beginner's perspective can often be more valuable than an expert's because it remembers what it's like to not understand something.

The Compound Benefits

1. Faster Learning

When you share what you're learning, you get feedback. People correct your misconceptions, suggest better approaches, and share related resources. This feedback loop accelerates learning far beyond what's possible in isolation.

2. Better Understanding

Explaining a concept forces you to understand it more deeply. You can't hand-wave through the parts that don't make sense when you're trying to explain them to someone else. Teaching reveals the gaps in your own knowledge.

3. Building Your Network

Sharing your learning journey attracts like-minded people. You'll connect with others who are on similar paths or who have walked the road before you. These connections often lead to opportunities, collaborations, and friendships.

4. Creating Your Own Reference

When you document your learning publicly, you create a searchable archive of your own growth. I can't count the number of times I've googled a problem only to find my own blog post from six months ago explaining exactly what I needed to remember.

Overcoming the Barriers

The biggest barrier to learning in public is psychological. Here are some common concerns and how to think about them:

"I'm Not Expert Enough"

You don't need to be an expert to be helpful. Some of the most valuable content comes from people who are just one step ahead of their audience. They remember what it's like to be confused and can explain things in a way that experts might take for granted.

"Someone Else Has Already Written About This"

Yes, and that's okay. Your perspective is unique. Maybe you explain it in a way that finally clicks for someone who didn't understand the other explanations. Maybe you connect it to different concepts or use different examples.

"I Might Be Wrong"

You probably will be sometimes, and that's perfect. Being corrected publicly is one of the fastest ways to learn. Most people are helpful rather than harsh when correcting honest mistakes.

Practical Steps to Start

If you're convinced but not sure where to begin, here are some low-pressure ways to start learning in public:

  1. Start a learning log. Write short notes about what you learned each day. Share the interesting bits on Twitter or LinkedIn.
  2. Document your setup. Share your development environment, tools, or workflows. This content is surprisingly popular and helpful.
  3. Explain it like I'm five. Take a complex concept you just learned and try to explain it as simply as possible.
  4. Share your resources. Curate the best tutorials, articles, or videos you found on a topic.
  5. Ask good questions. When you're stuck, ask thoughtful questions that include context and what you've already tried.
  6. Build in public. Share progress on side projects, even when they're messy or incomplete.

The Ripple Effect

When you learn in public, you contribute to a culture of openness and continuous learning. You make it okay for others to admit what they don't know. You help normalize the fact that everyone is constantly learning, regardless of their experience level.

Some of my most meaningful professional relationships started with a simple blog post or Twitter thread about something I was learning. People reached out with similar experiences, different perspectives, or just to say thanks. Those conversations led to job opportunities, collaborations, and friendships.

Making It Sustainable

Learning in public shouldn't feel like a burden. Here are some tips to make it sustainable:

  • Start small. A tweet or short note is better than a comprehensive blog post you never publish.
  • Be consistent. Regular small contributions compound better than sporadic large ones.
  • Find your medium. Some people prefer writing, others video, others code examples. Use whatever feels natural.
  • Don't overthink it. Share rough drafts and incomplete thoughts. Perfect is the enemy of done.
  • Engage with others. Learning in public is about community, not broadcasting into the void.

The Long View

Learning in public is an investment in your future self and your community. It builds your reputation as someone who cares about growth and isn't afraid to admit what they don't know. In an industry that changes as fast as technology, that's a superpower.

The goal isn't to look smart—it's to get smarter, and to help others do the same. Start today, start small, and see where it takes you.
